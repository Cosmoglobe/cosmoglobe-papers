\maketitle\abstracts{
The Cosmoglobe project is a global effort to jointly analyse complementary cosmological and astrophysical datasets, in order to better understand our Universe and its evolution. This paper describes the main goals and motivations of the project, as well as some of the main results that have been obtained so far and future prospects.

\section{Introduction}

The idea behind the Cosmoglobe project is very simple: if all telescopes are looking at the same sky, then, when analysed together, these should have an underlying signal in common. By jointly analysing these datasets, we can better constrain both this underlying signal, as well as any instrumental or otherwise systematic effect that could otherwise be mistaken for cosmological signal. This allows us to take advantage of the complementarity of the different datasets.

In order to do this, we use a Bayesian end-to-end analysis framework, called \Commander~\footnote{%Originally named this way as it was meant to be used in combination with the Master algorithm%~\cite{hivon:2002}
%, and after the, at the time, famous film: Master and Commander. Also meant to be an acronym for 
``Commander is an Optimal Monte-carlo Markov chAiN Driven EstimatoR''%.
}~\cite{eriksen:2004,seljebotn:2019,bp03}. \Commander\ is a Gibbs sampling code, which allows us to iteratively sample from the full joint posterior distribution of all the parameters in our model, including both the cosmological signal and the instrumental parameters. This allows us to propagate uncertainties in a consistent way, and to marginalize over any nuisance parameters. %A schema of the Cosmoglobe loop is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:cosmoglobe}.

Finally, we do it all over again, using what we have learned in the previous iteration to better calibrate the data, and so on, until we have converged to a stable solution and fully explored the viable parameter space.}\label{fig:cosmoglobe}

\subsection{\commanderone}

\commanderone, or, at the time, simply \Commander, was first developed as a means for power spectrum estimation for the high resolution maps produced by the \WMAP\ mission%~\cite{bennett:2013}
. It was later used as one of the main component separation frameworks for the analysis of the \Planck\ data~\cite{planck2014-a12}. A particularly useful feature of \commanderone\ is the ability to sample spatially variable spectral indices~\cite{fuskeland2014}. The \commanderone\ code was developed in Fortran and is not publicly available. \aism{should we use this as an opportunity to add commander 1 and 2 to the github?}

\subsection{\commandertwo}

\commandertwo\ is the second generation of the \Commander\ framework, designed to be able to do everything \commanderone\ could do, including now also support for multi-resolution datasets. The code \commandertwo\ code was also developed in Fortran and is also not publicly available.

\subsection{\commanderthree}

\Commander\ suffered a major rewrite in between its second and third generations, with \commanderthree\ now being able to perform end-to-end analysis, starting from the raw data, all the way to parameter estimation. All of the Cosmoglobe results available at the time of writing have been produced using \commanderthree. The \commanderthree\ code is written in Fortran and is publicaly available on GitHub~\footnote{\url{https://github.com/Cosmoglobe/Commander}}. It is also still maintained and developed at the time of writting.

\subsection{\commanderfour}
\commanderfour\ is the latest generation of the \Commander\ framework, which, once again, meant a major rewrite of the \Commander\ code. This was done in order to be able to efficiently parallelize the processing steps and to run on modern high performance computing architectures, so that we can handle larger, new generation datasets. %The differences between the architectures of \commanderthree\ and \commanderfour\ are outlined in Figure~\ref{fig:commander4}. 
While \commanderthree\ has a linear architecture, only being able to parallelize tasks within one node, \commanderfour\ is able to paralelize tasks across multiple nodes. \commanderfour\ is also designed to be more modular and flexible than its predecessors, specifically to allow new contributors to easily analyse their own datasets with it or even add new features. \commanderfour\ is currently being developed in Python and is publicaly available on GitHub~\footnote{\url{https://github.com/Cosmoglobe/Commander4}}.

\section{BeyondPlanck: Global Bayesian analysis of the Planck Low Frequency Instrument data}

Once the project period for \Planck\ was nearing its end, the \Planck\ analysis team was running the data through an iterative process, where each part of the analysis was performed by a different physical node and different people. As such, the process was slower than was needed to get enough iterations to be able to infer robust error bars onto the estimated cosmological parameters. With this in mind, the BeyondPlanck project~\cite{bp01} was created, along with the \commanderthree\ software: an outlet for the \Planck\ LFI data to be iteratively analysed in an end-to-end pipeline, jointly with other relevant datasets. In particular, the 353 and 857\,GHz frequency maps produced in the original analysis from the other instrument aboard \Planck, the High Frequency Instrument (HFI), in order to be able to characterize the thermal dust that is more clear at high frequencies, but still affects the low frequencies that LFI picks up, and the Haslam map was used as a way to constrain synchrotron emission. At the same time, maps in both temperature and polarization from the Ka through V bands produced in the \WMAP\ mission also helped the analysis, pushing on the idea that any instrument, independent of scanning strategy or any instrumental systematics, should be looking at the same sky.

An important product from the BeyondPlanck analysis was the estimation of the optical depth at reionization $\tau$, taking into account systematic effects that had previously been undiscovered and fully propagating the uncertainties from the raw data to the final cosmological parameters. As such, we find bigger uncertainties in the value of $\tau$, as well as a slightly higher value than in previous analyses%, as shown in the left side of Figure~\ref{fig:beyondplanck}
.

At the same time, a more complete sky model, ranging a larger frequency range than previously \Planck\ had is delivered, only able to be produced thanks to the power of joint analysis of multiple, complimentary datasets, in both temperature and polarization%, as shown in the right side of Figure~\ref{fig:beyondplanck}
.

\section{Data Release 1: Improved Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe maps through Bayesian end-to-end analysis}

Once the proof-of-concept BeyondPlanck analysis proved to be so successful, the Cosmoglobe project was born, and its first goal was to reanalyse the \WMAP\ raw data, \textit{together} with the \textit{raw} data from \Planck\ LFI, resulting in what we named the Cosmoglobe Data Release 1~\cite{watts2023_dr1}.

A particularly noteworthy result of DR1 are the new \WMAP\ frequency maps, %shown in Figure~\ref{fig:dr1},
shown in the overview paper and accessible on the Cosmoglobe website, which are now have better control of systematic effects, allowing us to exploit the full potential of the \WMAP\ polarization data, which was previously not possible.

\section{Data Release 2: Global Bayesian analysis of COBE-DIRBE}

With two successful analyses at the microwave frequencies, the Cosmoglobe team decided to shift its attention to the infrared frequency range, in order to be able to better characterize the astrophysical foregrounds at those frequencies, which, in turn, will also help us get cleaner (polarized) CMB maps and, thus, better estimation of the cosmological parameters that describe the beginning and evolution of the Universe.

In particular, the Cosmoglobe Data Release 2~\cite{CG02_01} focused on the joint analysis of the \COBE-DIRBE data, starting from the raw time-ordered data, together with higher frequency information from \Gaia, as well as templates based on data from \Planck\ HFI, \COBE-FIRAS, HI4PI, WHAM and Dame. This analysis resulted in improved zodiacal-light corrected maps, shown in the overview paper and accessible on the Cosmoglobe website, as well as a better characterization of the thermal dust emission, through the use of a new, simpler, physically motivated model.

\section{Current Status and Future Prospects}

Since its beginning, the Cosmoglobe project has aimed to bring the community together, not only through the analysis of different datasets together, but also through the sharing of knowledge and, of course, data~\cite{bp05}. As such, all of the data products developed by Cosmoglobe can be directly accessed through our website~\footnote{https://www.cosmoglobe.uio.no/}.

The development of \commanderfour\ means that datasets that were previously inaccessible to us, can now be analysed in a reasonable amount of time. As such, the Cosmoglobe team is currently actively working on the following projects, which we believe can highly benefit the community at large:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the re-analysis of \AKARI\ data, with particular focus on better zodiacal light modelling, as well as low level systematics, in order to characterize thermal dust emission at an extremely high angular resolution;
\item the re-analysis of \COBE-FIRAS data, with particular focus on thermometer calibration and glitch correction, looking to improve upon the current measurement of the CMB temperature, as well as the characterization of the CMB spectral distortions;
\item the re-analysis of \Planck\ HFI data, with particular focus on better control of systematic effects, in order to be able to exploit the full potential of the HFI polarization data;
\item the analysis of CHIPASS data, which will unlock the best measurements of synchrotron to date;
\item the analysis of Simons Observatory data, both in order to get the best high-resolution maps of the sky at millimeter wavelengths, as well as to unlock the first measurement of the tensor-to-scalar ratio $r$;
\item and the analysis of Fermi-LAT data, expanding our frequency range to the gamma-ray sky, and finding its correlation with the microwave and infrared sky.
\end{enumerate}

The Cosmoglobe team is also looking forward to the analysis of data from future experiments, such as LiteBIRD and FOSSIL, and is simultaneaously involved in the development of their simulations and data analysis pipelines.
